01 — DESIGN DIRECTION

Ledger-grade

Two traditions fused: the engraved print ledger — warm ink and paper, hairline rules, a serif that speaks only when the brand speaks — and the terminal — every amount, timestamp, and axis label set in monospace, exact to the cent, provenance one keystroke away.

Personality in four words: exact, calm, engraved, auditable. It should feel like a precision instrument you own, not a service you visit.

The one signature element is trust as furniture: every number on every surface can show its work. A dotted underline marks any auditable figure; a “SQL” chip on every widget reveals the exact query behind it; a persistent vault status line — encryption, file, row count, sync freshness — lives in the chrome like a terminal status bar. Trust is never a marketing claim here. It is a UI affordance you can click.

02 — COLOR SYSTEM

Ink, paper, brass

Warm near-neutrals — ink and paper, not gray — with one accent: brass, the coin. Not blue: blue is the category’s asserted-trust cliché, and this brand carries trust by behavior. Dark leads because the audience’s ambient environment is a dark editor and this product sits beside it; light is fully first-class and leads on the marketing site and in print.

Income/expense pair is teal-green vs. red-orange (distinguishable under deuteranopia), and sign is always carried redundantly: explicit +/− and ▲/▼, accounting convention (negative = expense).

03 — TYPOGRAPHY

Serif speaks, sans works, mono counts

Three faces, three jobs. All three are SIL Open Font License — bundleable in an AGPL product and self-hosted: a no-telemetry product does not phone a font CDN. The hard rule: money is always mono. Every amount, everywhere, in tabular JetBrains Mono — that is what makes dense financial tables align to the cent, and it is half the brand’s voice.

05 — CHART GRAMMAR

Charts are the soul; honesty is the style

BI-grade means the chart would survive scrutiny in a Hex notebook. Eight rules, no exceptions. Exemplars live in the dashboard below (§9).

1  Axes: no axis strokes. The zero baseline is the only emphasized rule. If zero is off-scale, the chart says so in the corner — never silently.
2  Grid: horizontal hairlines only, five at most. Never vertical gridlines — the crosshair does that job on demand.
3  Labels: JetBrains Mono, 11px, text/faint. Currency abbreviated on axes ($425K, $1.2M); full precision reserved for tooltips.
4  Honesty: linear interpolation only — a smoothed spline is a small lie about your money. Missing data renders as a gap, never bridged.
5  Tooltips: a ledger row — mono, date first, exact to the cent. The crosshair snaps to real data points only, never interpolated positions.
6  Color: single series = brass. Multi-series = chart/1–8 in order, six max before grouping to “Other.” Income/expense charts use the semantic pair, zero-anchored, signed above/below the baseline.
7  Fills: area fill at ≤8% of its stroke color. No gradients anywhere else. No glow, no rounded line caps on bars.
8  Provenance: every chart carries a SQL chip. If a widget can’t state its query, it doesn’t ship.
06 — ICONOGRAPHY

Drafting-table strokes

20×20 grid, 1.5px stroke, squared caps, no fills, one weight. Metaphors are literal — a table for tables, a key for encryption — never decorative. Banned: coins raining, sparkles, magic wands. The AI surface is marked by the terminal caret ▸_, not ✨ — “digital magic” here means craftsmanship, and the honest symbol of asking a machine is a prompt.

Ship as a custom-drawn set on this grammar; where a stock icon is unavoidable, restroke it to spec.

07 — VOICE & MICROCOPY

Numbers first, verbs second

No exclamation points. No “oops.” No superlatives — the strategy forbids hype, so the copy does too. Errors state the fact, then the next action. Scary words are said plainly: encrypted, deleted, irreversible. Humor budget: empty states and the CLI only — dry, one line, never near money, errors, or security.

08 — LOGO & MASCOT

The slot, not the swim

MoneyBin
MoneyBin
mascot exploration — commission an illustrator on the rules at right

Mark: the coin and the slot. A vault-square with a coin poised above its slot — the instant before deposit. It says exactly what the product is (one bin, everything goes in, nothing leaks out), reads at 16px, and shares no anatomy with any duck swimming in gold. The wordmark is Newsreader semibold.

Mascot: “Bill,” demoted to the edges. The duck is a documentation and CLI character, never app chrome and never the logo. Construction rules that keep him original: geometric flat construction on the icon grid; he guards the bin, never bathes in it; no coin piles, no diving, no top hat, cane, or spats; no wealth-fantasy poses at all. In the CLI he may appear as ASCII. The early sketch (duck splashing in a coin crate) is respectfully retired — splashing in money is the exact silhouette the IP caution names.

Trust is carried by provenance, not by charm — so the brand survives even if the duck never ships.

10 — RATIONALE: CONSIDERED & REJECTED

The roads not taken

Dark blue/violet SaaS dashboard. The default output of every tool and template in 2026. This audience recognizes it instantly and — per the brief — distrusts it. Rejected on sight; warm ink neutrals chosen instead.
Blue as the trust color. The category cliché: trust asserted by hue. Here trust is proven by the provenance system, freeing the accent budget for brass — the coin, ownable and warm. The risk (brass reads “crypto luxury”) is managed by restraint: brass never fills large areas.
Inter + neutral-gray component kit. Reads as templated shadcn-default to exactly the people MoneyBin is for. Schibsted Grotesk carries the same utility without the fingerprint.
Terminal cosplay (phosphor green on black). Flatters the engineer audience but fails two tests: consumer users arrive later and must be accommodated, and BI credibility requires typographic range a terminal aesthetic can’t offer. We kept the terminal’s honesty (mono data, status line) and left the costume.
Duck-forward playful brand. A mascot-led identity spends the trust budget on charm, and swims straight toward the Disney silhouette. The duck is demoted to docs and CLI under strict construction rules; the mark is the slot instead.
Smoothed gradient area charts. The polished-consumer look (and the prettiest option). Rejected as dishonest at the data layer: splines invent values between your data points. Grammar rule 4 forbids it — and that refusal is the brand.
Light-first. Seriously considered — the ledger heritage argues for paper, and dense reading is easier on light. Dark leads because the product lives beside dark editors and charts carry more contrast there; light ships first-class the same day, and leads on marketing and print.
A trendier serif (Fraunces et al.). Distinctive two years ago, default-trendy now. Newsreader is editorial, quiet, and optically sized — gravitas without fashion. All faces OFL so an AGPL product can bundle and self-host them.
